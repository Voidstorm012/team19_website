What is this project about?

This project is part of GALLANT‚Äôs wider efforts to promote sustainable travel in Glasgow and tracking the CO‚ÇÇ offsets achieved when users choose walking or cycling over motorised transport. CommuniMap is a digital platform that allows users to log walking and cycling routes, supporting low-carbon travel habits and community research. Our work explores how to enhance CommuniMap through gamification to make the experience more engaging. We framed the design process around a clear problem of low long-term user engagement, and used iterative design thinking and stakeholder input to develop and refine features like virtual pets, rewards, and streak systems. These additions are designed to create a more motivating and meaningful user experience.

The Problem

From our stakeholder discussions, we learned that CommuniMap‚Äôs current gamification features such as badges and a leaderboard are too hidden and fail to engage users. To encourage consistent contributions, gamification needs to be fun, visible, and habit-forming, taking inspiration from successful apps like Duolingo with tiered leaderboards, streak systems, and group achievements. While vouchers have been effective in the past, they are not sustainable as the sole motivator, so creative, non-monetary rewards such as in-app recognition and unlockable milestones should also be considered. Above all, features should remain simple, easy to find, and intuitive to use to maximise their impact.


Through further research, stakeholder discussions, and hands-on exploration of the CommuniMap app, we identified several key issues that limit user engagement and long-term participation. These challenges highlight areas where the platform could be improved to better motivate active travel and increase the visibility of users‚Äô impact.

  • üìç
    Low Visibility of Progress & Impact: Key motivators such as achievements, leaderboards, and CO‚ÇÇ savings are hidden or difficult to access, reducing their effectiveness in encouraging participation.
  • üéÆ
    Uninspiring Gamification: Existing game elements are minimal and fail to provide a sense of fun, progression, or long-term engagement for users.
  • üíö
    Lack of Emotional Connection: The platform offers no personalised or playful features, such as virtual companions, that can build lasting relationships between users and the app.
  • üó∫Ô∏è
    Limited Exploration Appeal: The map interface is functional but lacks engaging or immersive elements to encourage exploration and discovery.
  • üìç
    Weak Community Engagement: Lack of promoting participation in local events, collaboration between users, or shared community goals.

Why This Matters

Engagement isn't just about making the app more enjoyable - it directly impacts Glasgow's sustainability goals. When users actively log their journeys, they contribute to a bigger picture: understanding and reducing CO‚ÇÇ emissions across the city.

1
Lower engagement means less real-world data on low-carbon travel patterns, weakening policy decisions.
2

3

4
Potential behavior change toward active travel diminishes

By solving these engagement challenges, we're not just improving an app - we're supporting Glasgow's transition to net-zero carbon emissions by empowering residents to track and understand their sustainable travel choices.

Our Proposed Solution

To address the challenges identified through stakeholder discussions and app exploration, we designed a set of gamified features that enhance engagement, make progress visible, and reward sustainable travel habits. These solutions combine emotional connection, tangible incentives, and immersive experiences to keep users motivated over the long term.


üêæ Virtual Pet System

+

Engage users with a virtual companion that grows and evolves based on their sustainable travel choices.

  • Pet health tied to sustainable transport usage
  • Different pet species unlock with milestones
  • Social sharing of pet achievements

Why this works

Virtual companions provide a sense of responsibility and emotional attachment. Users are more likely to stay engaged when they feel invested in the progress and well-being of something they care about, even digitally. This taps into long-term motivation beyond just extrinsic rewards.

üí∞ Currency & Rewards

+

Earn virtual currency through active travel that can be spent on pet customizations and real-world rewards such as Nextbike vouchers.

  • Points earned per kilometer of sustainable travel
  • Bonus multipliers for consistent usage
  • Seasonal challenges with special rewards

Why this works

Reward systems create positive reinforcement loops. Users are encouraged to build sustainable travel habits when there‚Äôs a tangible payoff for consistency. A virtual currency is flexible, scalable, and allows for both short-term gratification and long-term goals, while linking to real-world incentives like bike vouchers strengthens motivation.

üèÜ Social Recognition & Achievements

+

Celebrate and showcase user milestones through visible achievements, public leaderboards, and shareable progress summaries.

  • Achievement badges for key milestones (e.g., 100km travelled, 30-day streak)
  • Tiered leaderboard rankings with seasonal resets
  • Shareable profile cards displaying CO‚ÇÇ saved and personal bests

Why this works

Social recognition taps into intrinsic motivation by making progress public and valued. Users are more likely to stay active when they can compare with peers, celebrate wins, and contribute to group achievements.

üó∫Ô∏è 2D/3D Hybrid Map

+

Interactive mapping interface that combines the clarity of 2D with the engagement of 3D elements.

  • Toggle between 2D efficiency and 3D exploration
  • AR elements for real-world navigation

Why this works

A hybrid map supports both clarity and engagement. The 2D view offers usability and speed for everyday tracking, while the 3D/AR view makes exploration feel immersive and gamified. This balances utility with enjoyment.

üìç Event System

+

Dynamic event pop-up and location-based challenges that connect digital engagement with real-world activities.

  • Push pop-up for ongoing challenges and local events
  • Interactive event pins displayed directly on the map interface
  • Reward system for attending workshops and visiting specific locations
  • Community-driven event creation and participation tracking

Why this works

Events give users a reason to return. Location-based and time-limited activities create urgency and excitement, while also promoting real-world movement. It supports community engagement and makes digital participation feel more grounded in the physical world.

üß† Ideation

We explored several gamified and sustainability-focused apps to identify best practices and gaps. Duolingo inspired our use of tiered leaderboards and streak systems to encourage daily engagement. Pok√©mon Go demonstrated how 2D/3D hybrid maps and location-based interactions can turn movement into an immersive experience. LumiHealth showed the effectiveness of daily challenges and earning coins that can be redeemed for real-world rewards. However, most platforms lacked visible CO‚ÇÇ impact gaps we aimed to address with CommuniMap.

We began the project by interpreting the brief through the lens of CO‚ÇÇ tracking. Initially, we focused on researching the best ways to calculate and visualize carbon savings, thinking this would create the most impact. However, after speaking with our stakeholder, we realised this wasn't the app‚Äôs core problem.

The real issue was around engagement. Features like badges and leaderboards already existed, but were buried or felt disconnected from the user's experience. This shifted our focus: instead of adding new elements blindly, we asked ourselves how we could make features feel purposeful, visible, and emotionally meaningful.

We explored existing gamification approaches ‚Äî like reward loops, pet companions, social progression ‚Äî and analysed why some failed while others worked. From this, we drafted several ideas before narrowing it down to five final features that best balanced motivation, usability, and feasibility.

Once we understood that the core issue wasn‚Äôt just a lack of features, but the lack of visible and meaningful engagement, we aligned on a clearer direction: to enhance intuitiveness and motivation without overwhelming users.

We created low-fidelity mockups and interactive Figma screens to prototype ideas such as app-following pets, daily streaks, visible rewards, and achievement popups. These ideas aimed to make sustainable actions feel rewarding in a more obvious, interactive way.

This wasn‚Äôt just about designing visuals, building prototypes helped us figure out what actually felt intuitive, fun, or confusing. Some concepts looked good on paper but fell flat in testing. Interestingly, simpler elements like visible streak counters, progress feedback, or small positive reinforcements turned out to be more effective than larger, more complex ideas.

We shared our designs with our stakeholder and gathered feedback. This helped us refine the direction further and focus on what really worked.

Reflection on Interactions with Partners

Our interactions with Sarah from the GALLANT team was central to shaping our direction, clearing ambiguities, and driving us toward an evidence-based, feasible, and engaging solution. The conversations didn‚Äôt just help us refine a prototype, they fundamentally transformed our understanding of what our role was, what constraints existed, and what possibilities we could pursue within and beyond those constraints.

From Ambiguity to Clarity

Before our first meeting with Sarah, we had developed a general idea that our solution should increase user engagement and long-term participation in CommuniMap. However, the scope was unclear: Were we improving the existing app? Proposing an entirely new platform? Should we research carbon dioxide calculations? Were there existing methods they preferred? These foundational uncertainties were addressed immediately during the first stakeholder meeting.

We learned that the app was developed externally and that direct backend access or structural changes were not permitted. ‚ÄúWe can't make any changes to this,‚Äù Sarah explained about the Spotteron platform, ‚ÄúI‚Äôd get in trouble if I gave you the login.‚Äù This clarified that our focus should be on conceptual enhancements, ideally imagined in a ‚Äúfuture version‚Äù of the platform, assuming unlimited time and budget. This subtle but important constraint narrowed our approach while simultaneously encouraging creative freedom within realistic boundaries.

It also helped us see that the deliverable wasn‚Äôt a deployable product; it was a vision of what could be, should the infrastructure and funding become available. We were free to explore bold ideas, map out ideal user journeys, and reimagine the app‚Äôs potential through sketches, mockups, and interaction flows. This early boundary-setting allowed us to confidently shift from development mode to conceptual design mode and better frame our proposal‚Äôs purpose.

Co-Exploring Ideas Together

A significant part of our engagement with Sarah was the co-exploration of ideas, where feedback loops occurred in real-time. Rather than presenting fixed features, we collaboratively discussed options such as pet-based progression systems, streak mechanics with improved leaderboard, and gamified rewards.

For example, when we showed our initial concept, drawing inspiration from Pok√©mon Go with a pet cow avatar, challenges, and point-based CO‚ÇÇ tracking, we were met with enthusiasm but also a balanced perspective. Sarah helped us weigh the trade-offs: ‚ÄúIf you're too forward with the educational element, people get bored,‚Äù she noted, but ‚Äúif you're too playful, people may lose sight of the core purpose.‚Äù She also raised a critical point: what if some users simply wanted to use the app as a tool, not as a game? This prompted us to consider different user types and design with flexibility in mind, ensuring the experience could remain functional and meaningful even for those not motivated by gamification.

This conversation opened space to explore the delicate balance between engagement and intention. Together, we asked: Could a pet system foster emotional investment without turning the app into a game? Would leaderboards encourage healthy competition or alienate users who weren‚Äôt as active? These were not hypothetical questions‚Äîwe explored them through back-and-forth discussion, testing not just what sounded good, but what aligned with the behavioural goals of the project.

Sarah offered alternative motivators we hadn‚Äôt initially considered, such as an event pop-up tab when users login, especially helpful since ongoing events were only visible on the map and often overlooked by users. She affirmed our ideas of real-world incentives, such as bike rental vouchers, could strongly encourage cycling and reinforce the app‚Äôs sustainability goals. This broadened our understanding of motivation beyond digital feedback and into real-world rewards. These insights directly shaped how we structured the points, streaks, and event-triggered features in our solution.

Insights That Shaped Our Design

One of the biggest takeaways from our conversations was how varied user motivations could be. Some users are data-driven and want to contribute meaningfully. Others might use the app once and abandon it if they don‚Äôt find it engaging. ‚ÄúThe biggest challenge is retention,‚Äù she said. ‚ÄúSome will open it once, think it‚Äôs boring, and never open it again.‚Äù

This prompted us to think deeply about first impressions, daily engagement cues, and meaningful longevity. From this, we designed subtle nudges like daily streak icons, dynamic pets, and challenge reminders. These weren‚Äôt just features, they were direct responses to our stakeholder‚Äôs concern that ‚Äúthe gamification element is buried‚Äù and should be more visible. We translated this feedback into a more prominent user interface that foregrounds rewards and progress the moment the user opens the app.

Another critical insight was around feasibility. While we floated ideas like AR-based pet interaction, Sarah grounded the discussion: ‚ÄúThat would be a fun concept, but way over the top, not feasible.‚Äù (She did say it would be really cool.) Rather than dismissing the idea, she reframed it, suggesting we keep the concept but strip it down to its emotionally resonant core, like animations and small celebratory effects. This helped us build a design that feels alive and interactive, even if limited by technical feasibility.

Mutual Learning and Respectful Collaboration

What stood out most about the interaction with Sarah was the tone of mutual respect and open-minded collaboration. Instead of critiquing from a distance, Sarah engaged deeply with our prototypes, even suggesting improvements to UI layout, leaderboard visibility, and content accessibility. She shared brand guidelines, explained the app‚Äôs architectural limitations, and offered creative workarounds such as integrating our prototype into a companion site or exploring reward partnerships with local vendors.

This partnership gave us more than feedback, it gave us a design lens rooted in real users, system constraints, and long-term impact. It also helped us appreciate the importance of clearly defined assumptions. For example, we learned that CO‚ÇÇ metrics could vary widely based on transport mode, fuel type, and location. As Sarah put it, ‚ÄúThere‚Äôs a lot; it depends on car type, size, shape, and more.‚Äù Instead of trying to find a perfect formula, she advised us to use an average of the available average metrics and explain it clearly. This reinforced an important insight: on community-driven platforms, transparency often matters more than technical precision.

Translating Reflection into Action

Through our interactions, several core themes emerged that directly influenced our final prototype:

  • Clarifying assumptions early allows creativity to flourish within defined boundaries
  • Gamification is only effective when aligned with user intention and context
  • User retention relies on clarity, immediacy, and feedback, not just features
  • Designing with stakeholders leads to stronger outcomes than designing for them

These weren‚Äôt just theoretical learnings, they were translated into specific design changes, such as simplifying UI icons, improving leaderboard access and focusing rewards on both fun and function. Our final design, while playful, is grounded in behavioural insight, stakeholder context, and community science values.

Ultimately, our collaboration with GALLANT shaped not just our product, but our entire design process. We moved from assumption to alignment, from isolated ideation to shared ownership, and from feature-driven design to purpose-driven interaction. Sarah didn‚Äôt simply guide us, she partnered with us in building a solution that was not only engaging, but meaningful, implementable, and grounded in the lived reality of both users and system constraints.

This experience has deepened our confidence in the power of collaborative design, and in the value of listening deeply, iterating honestly, and staying flexible in the face of evolving feedback. It is this process and not just the final product, that we are most proud of.

What We Are Proud Of

Clarity of Focus Through Iteration

One of the accomplishments we are most proud of was how our team was able to pivot from a fun-focused concept to a purpose-driven experience. Initially inspired by gamification-heavy models like Pok√©mon Go, our early ideas leaned toward entertaining features like interactive AR pets, collectible creatures and fighting of ‚Äúmonsters‚Äù. But through discussions with our stakeholder, we came to recognize that excessive playfulness could dilute the core goal of encouraging climate-positive travel.

Instead of discarding the idea entirely, we refined it by retaining a virtual pet system but grounding it in real-world behaviour change. For instance, the pet can be interacted with by "touching" it, which will subsequently produce a sound.. We learned to strike a balance between delight and direction, transforming a playful concept into a motivational tool.

Responsive, Evidence-Based Design

We‚Äôre proud of how we allowed stakeholder input and user insight to shape our design decisions. Our design was never static; each round of feedback led to deliberate, documented adjustments, such as simplifying UI icons, clarifying leaderboard access, and incorporating reward systems tied to verified behaviours. We didn‚Äôt just listen, we acted on feedback provided by our stakeholder.

For example, when the stakeholder pointed out that buried features (like leaderboards and badge systems) currently lack visibility in the live app, we reorganized our layout to make those elements more prominent. Our solution emerged as a result of responsiveness, not rigid planning, and that adaptability is something we deeply value.

Engagement with System Constraints

Rather than being frustrated by limitations (like not having backend access or Spotteron‚Äôs locked infrastructure), we saw them as design constraints to work within creatively. We focused on what we could control: crafting a meaningful conceptual design layer, imagining ideal future features, and aligning them with user motivation and feasibility.

For example, even though we couldn‚Äôt implement AR or real-time CO‚ÇÇ tracking, we mocked up plausible interactions and clearly articulated the assumptions and metrics behind them. Our prototype respects real-world constraints while offering a compelling vision of future possibilities.

Team Collaboration and Shared Ownership

We are also proud of our internal teamwork. Each member contributed unique perspectives, whether in UI design, concept framing, user behaviour, or stakeholder interaction. We embraced shared ownership of both successes and struggles, working cohesively through brainstorming sessions, feedback reviews, and content creation. Our ability to adapt to feedback and collectively defend or revise ideas shows maturity and respect across the team.

What We Are Not Proud Of

Over-Investment in Early Concepts

A key learning point was how we initially became overly invested in features like AR pets, evolving maps, and fully interactive avatars. While these ideas were exciting, we spent time and creative energy developing them before properly assessing feasibility. In hindsight, we could have tested assumptions earlier, explored simpler solutions, and saved effort for refining core experiences.

Although we eventually adjusted course, the early momentum around non-viable ideas delayed progress in more critical areas such as UI clarity, onboarding flows, and habit-forming feedback mechanisms.

Limited External User Testing

While we had rich interactions with our stakeholder, our design would have benefited from broader user validation outside of GALLANT. Time constraints and scoping challenges meant we were unable to conduct field testing or broader surveys. This limited our ability to gauge how real users, especially those unfamiliar with community science projects, would engage with the app.

Though we made informed assumptions using behavioural frameworks, real-world testing would have validated (or challenged) key flows like streak progression, pet motivation, and reward incentives. It‚Äôs a gap we recognize and would prioritize in future iterations.

Visual Identity Consistency

In the early stages, our UI choices were guided more by creativity than consistency. While we eventually adopted GALLANT‚Äôs branding and visual guidelines, this decision came later in the process. Had we begun with clearer brand alignment, we could have streamlined decision-making around typography, layout, and colour contrast earlier‚Äîensuring greater cohesion from the start.
